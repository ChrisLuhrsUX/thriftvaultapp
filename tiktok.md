# ThriftVault Short-Form Video Content Strategy
### Complete Playbook for TikTok, Reels & YouTube Shorts

---

## 1. CURRENT TRENDS & FORMATS (March 2026)

### Trending Audio & Formats in Thrift/Reseller Niche

**Audio Styles Performing Best Right Now:**
- **Hyperpop/sped-up audio** layered under satisfying "reveal" moments — the fast tempo matches the scroll energy of Gen Z and creates urgency
- **Lo-fi cash register or "cha-ching" sound effects** synced to profit numbers appearing on screen — extremely high save rate in reseller content
- **Silence + dramatic single sound effect** (a "ping," a beep, a notification sound) — counterintuitive but stops scrollers because the algorithm has trained users to expect noise, so silence creates pattern interruption
- **Trending audio remixed with thrift-specific text overlays** — you don't need to speak; your captions do the selling while a viral sound carries the video into the algorithm
- **Voice memo aesthetic audio** — raw, unedited voice recordings layered with text are outperforming polished voiceovers because they feel authentic

**Editing Styles Dominating:**
- **"Blink cut" editing** — hard cuts every 1.5–2 seconds with no transitions, creating an almost anxious pace that prevents thumb stops. Every frame must contain new information.
- **Green screen reaction format** — pointing at a screenshot of a sold listing, a scan result, or a profit dashboard while text overlays explain the story
- **Text-first POV** — 100% text on a B-roll background (thrift rack, a pile of clothes, a scanning hand), zero face, zero voice. This format performs especially well for founders who aren't comfortable on camera yet.
- **Before/After wipe transitions** — thrift store item on the left, sold listing screenshot on the right, revealed by a swipe or wipe at the midpoint
- **Countdown format** — "3 items I scanned, #3 will break your brain" shown as numbered text cuts. Forces completion.

**What's Stopping Scrollers Specifically in This Community:**
The single biggest pattern-interrupt for reseller audiences right now is a **number that seems wrong.** A price tag showing $1.99 next to a resale value of $140 makes the brain short-circuit and demands explanation. Lead with the absurdity of the gap, then explain it. The human brain is wired to resolve cognitive dissonance — if you show something that doesn't compute, people will watch the entire video to make it make sense.

Other proven scroll-stoppers in this niche: a brand logo they recognize in an unexpected context (a Prada tag at Goodwill makes them stop cold), a before/after resale profit that looks like a typo, and a question that implies they've been leaving money on the table ("You're scanning this wrong").

**Highest Save & Share Rate Content Types:**
- **Educational "cheat sheets"** (brands to look for, hidden labels, red flag stitching) — saved as a reference resource
- **Profit reveal videos** — shared to other resellers as proof of concept or aspiration
- **Scan reveal format** — shared because viewers want their followers to guess the value before the reveal
- **"I didn't know that"** moments — any video where a viewer learns something surprising gets shared to a friend with "did you know this??"

## 2. CONTENT PILLARS (20+ Video Ideas Each)

### PILLAR 1: Scan Reveals — Suspense → Payoff

The entire format is: show the item, build suspense (hold the scan moment one beat longer than comfortable), show the result. Silence + a single "ping" sound when the result appears is your most effective audio choice here.

1. Find a $2.99 Ralph Lauren polo, hold it up to camera, open app, slow pan to the scan screen, let the AI result appear in real time — result shows $45–65 resale. End on the number, no explanation needed.
2. Mystery bag from Goodwill bins (the $1.50/lb bins). Pull out one item, scan it immediately without inspecting it. Let the AI name what it is. The reveal IS the content.
3. Grandma's estate sale coat — looks like nothing, but it's a vintage Pendleton wool blazer. Scan before showing the tag. Text overlay: "I almost walked past this."
4. Scan a fast-fashion item (Shein, Fashion Nova label visible) to show the app returning a low or $0 resale value. Educational but framed as a scan reveal. "The app said what we all already knew."
5. Find something with no visible brand label — torn out, faded, or missing. Scan the item itself (fabric, buttons, silhouette). Let the AI attempt to identify it. Even a "low confidence" result is interesting content.
6. Thrift a beat-up leather jacket, scan it, reveal it's a brand worth restoring. Text: "Worth the clean-up?" Poll sticker drives engagement.
7. Scan three items back to back with no commentary. Just hand, scan, result. Satisfying rhythm. Sped-up audio. Blink cuts. The repetition IS the hook.
8. Scan something you've already sold — show the actual sold price vs. what the app predicted. If it's accurate, that's a trust-builder. If you beat the estimate, that's a brag.
9. "I scanned everything in this rack" — time-lapse of scanning 10 items, then end on the one item that returned the highest value. Make viewers wait for the payoff.
10. Scan a pairs of shoes with no brand visible. App identifies them as vintage Nike Cortez. Paid $3.99. Resale: $80–120. End on the number.
11. Scan a "luxury feel" item that turns out to be a knockoff or a low-value brand. Subverts expectations. Relatable for beginners who got burned.
12. Scan at the moment of decision — you're at the rack, you're unsure, you scan before deciding to buy. The app says yes or no. That real-time uncertainty is compelling.
13. "Scan vs. my gut" — show your gut guess as a text overlay, then scan, reveal who was right. Creates a game the viewer plays along with.
14. Find a sealed vintage graphic tee still in a plastic bag. Scan before opening fully. Reveal.
15. Scan a children's toy, costume, or random household item that shows surprisingly high resale value. Unexpected categories perform well because viewers learn something.
16. Scan a handbag with no visible logo (logo lining worn, exterior clean). Let the AI identify the house. Paid $6, resale $200+.
17. Scan the same item three weeks apart — show that resale values change seasonally. Educational and demonstrates app utility.
18. "Worst scan of the week" — item you were sure was valuable, app returned $3. Honesty builds trust faster than always showing wins.
19. Nighttime Goodwill run, low-light scanning. Gritty aesthetic. Shows the app works in real-world thrift store lighting, which is terrible.
20. Scan a full outfit head to toe — jacket, pants, shirt, shoes — and show cumulative total paid vs. cumulative resale potential. End on the net flip profit.
21. Scan something absurd — a $0.50 paperback that turns out to be a first edition worth $40 on eBay. Niche but extremely shareable.
22. Let a friend or family member pick something they think is worthless, then scan it together on camera. Reaction content without needing to perform.

### PILLAR 2: Profit Flexes — Cost vs. Sold

Format note: every video in this pillar should have a physical or visual "before" (receipt, haul spread, price tags) and a digital "after" (sold screenshots, profit dashboard in app). The contrast is the content.

1. Spread an entire thrift haul on a white background. Show total tags visible. Cut to the app's inventory screen showing total paid. Cut to sold total. One-second gap between each number for the brain to process.
2. Hold up a price tag: $4.99. Hold up a sold notification: $78. Two frames. Done. No words necessary.
3. "January haul report" — open the app's monthly summary screen. Let the profit number load. React with one facial expression (no words). Your face IS the caption.
4. Stack the physical items you bought in one week into a pile. Write total cost on a whiteboard or sticky note. Cut to app. Cut to sold total. Satisfying.
5. Receipt close-up — total $23.47 for seven items. Then sold screenshots stacked. Then app total. 10 seconds.
6. "My worst flip ever" — something you paid $15 for that sold for $11. Showing losses builds more trust than only showing wins, and gets more comments.
7. Live unboxing of a sold package — show the item going in, the shipping label, then cut to the profit logged in the app. Satisfying completion loop.
8. 90-day profit graph in the app. No context. Just the line going up. Text: "Started March 1st."
9. Side-by-side: thrift store dressing room mirror selfie in the item vs. the listing photo vs. the sold notification. Three-frame story.
10. "These 5 items cost me $18 total" — show them, then show them all sold, then show the $94 app total. Viewer math is part of the engagement.
11. "I almost didn't buy this" — show the item, the $3.99 tag, then the sold notification for $55. Regret/relief is a strong emotional pull.
12. Flip from thrift to listed in under 24 hours — time-stamp showing purchase, then sold notification timestamp. Speed is aspirational.
13. Show the app's "cost per item average" metric alongside your "average sale price." The gap is the proof.
14. "My most profitable brand this month" — app sorted by brand, reveal the winner. Community will debate in comments.
15. Open the app and scroll through your sold inventory. Let the numbers blur past. End on a total. The motion of scrolling through profit feels abundant.
16. "This is what $200 at the thrift store can become" — starting inventory cost vs. projected total resale. Forward-looking and aspirational.
17. "I spent $0 this week" — show how previously purchased inventory continuing to sell logs new profit without new spending. Passive income angle.
18. Film your phone notification screen: three Depop "item sold" notifications in a row. Caption: "It's happening."
19. Show the "profit per hour" calculation — if you spent 2 hours thrifting and made $80 net profit, that's $40/hour. The hourly rate framing makes it aspirational.
20. "Before I had this app I just guessed" — split screen of a handwritten notebook with numbers vs. the clean app dashboard. Pain vs. solution.
21. New Year / new month first haul profit — timing content to month starts makes it feel like a fresh start viewers want to join.

### PILLAR 3: Education — Brands, Red Flags, Pricing Tips

Format: fast text overlays, high information density, designed to be saved. Every video should have a clear "save this" prompt because saved videos tell the algorithm it's high-value content.

1. "3 brands to look for at Goodwill this week" — three blink cuts, each showing a label close-up with the brand name in text overlay and a resale range. 9 seconds.
2. "The stitching tells you everything" — show two garments side by side, one real, one fake, stitching close-up. No audio needed. Text: "Count the stitches per inch. Real vs. fake."
3. "These letters on a tag mean it's vintage" — show care label symbols that indicate pre-1971 or pre-1994 manufacture. This is extremely high save-rate content.
4. "Red flags that mean don't buy it" — odor indicator, pilling pattern, zipper brand that signals fast fashion. Text bullets, 3 seconds each, hard cuts.
5. "The $3 rack is where the money is" — explain why the cheap rack gets less competition. Counter-intuitive content gets saved.
6. "Scan this part of the shoe first" — show the interior tongue label vs. the box label. AI scans work better with certain reference points. Educational about the app and the skill.
7. "This Goodwill mistake costs you $30 per visit" — buying items with visible damage that can't be corrected. Show the damage types quickly: broken zippers, missing buttons, heat press peeling.
8. "Depop vs. eBay vs. Poshmark — where does THIS sell fastest?" — pick one item category and show the right platform for it. Immediately practical.
9. "The difference between consignment pricing and resale pricing" — 30 seconds of education compressed to 10 seconds with blink cuts.
10. "These 5 brands are overpriced at the thrift store right now" — brands like Lululemon that thrift stores have figured out the value of. Saves people from overpaying.
11. "Scan the tag, not the item" — explain that for branded goods, scanning the label gives more accurate results than scanning the garment. App tip that also educates.
12. "Linen vs. polyester — how to feel the difference in 3 seconds" — tactile education with close-up video of the texture test.
13. "Shoes: check this before anything else" — show the outsole wear pattern. A heel worn flat on one side means structural issues that can't be resold easily.
14. "The Goodwill color tag system explained" — for beginners. This gets massive saves because it's a legitimate mystery to new thrifters.
15. "Vintage Levi's dating guide" — the red tab vs. orange tab vs. no tab timeline. Shows in 8 seconds with close-up cuts to the actual tabs.
16. "This is why your listings aren't selling" — photos taken in bad lighting next to photos in natural light, same item. Single cause, single fix.
17. "The $10 equipment that changed my resale game" — cheap ring light, white foam board photo backdrop. Tools that improve listings.
18. "The 10% rule for thrift pricing" — only buy if the item sells for at least 10x what you're paying. Quick visual math.
19. "Cashmere test in 2 seconds" — the pilling texture test. Pure cashmere pills differently. Show it visually with two scarves.
20. "Never buy [brand] at the thrift unless you see [this label variation]" — brand-specific authenticity signals. Pick a different brand for each video in this series.
21. "Hidden pockets = vintage signal" — structural tells that indicate a garment predates modern manufacturing shortcuts. Very shareable "I had no idea" content.
22. "The app flagged this as low value — here's why it was right" — use the AI's assessment to explain resale market dynamics for a specific category.

### PILLAR 4: Engagement Bait — Comment-Driven Series

Format: pose a question that has no single right answer, or has a surprising right answer. Comments = algorithm signal. Every video should have a clear prompt to vote, answer, or debate in comments.

1. "Scan it or skip it?" — show a thrift item, show the price tag, cut to black before showing the scan result. Text: "What would you do? Comment ⬆️ scan or ⬇️ skip." Post the result in the next video.
2. "Guess the resale value" — show the item and the thrift price, hold on a question mark before revealing the scan. Comments flood with guesses before you post the answer as a reply.
3. "Would you buy this for $12.99?" — show an item that's clearly worth $12.99 to buy but iffy to flip. Genuine debate in comments.
4. "Hot take: [controversial reseller opinion]" — examples: "Poshmark is dying," "Depop fees aren't worth it anymore," "Bins thrifting is overrated." No need to be right — need to be provocative.
5. "Rate my pricing — am I undercharging?" — show a listing price, show comps on screen, ask comments to judge. Extremely relatable for beginners.
6. "This or this?" — two thrift finds, same price. Comments argue for their pick. You reveal what the app said about each.
7. "Comment a brand and I'll tell you if it's worth thrifting right now" — one video that promises a series. Build commitment before you post the follow-ups.
8. "Proof that [city]'s thrift stores are [better/worse] than [other city]" — regional pride and rivalry is genuinely engaging. Local communities will share it.
9. "What would you price this?" — show a found item, solicit comment prices, then reveal your actual listing price in the comments. The discrepancy drives thread engagement.
10. "I'm returning this — or am I?" — show an item you're unsure about. Ask for verdicts. Don't reveal the decision in the video, only in comments.
11. "The worst advice I see in reseller TikTok" — leave it open-ended. Comments will add their own worst advice and build a thread.
12. "Am I the only one who does this?" — show a quirky scanning or thrifting habit. Comments confirm or debate. Belonging-seeking behavior in comments.
13. "Tell me your biggest thrift flip loss in the comments — I'll start" — community sharing lowers the barrier for vulnerability and builds a thread.
14. "Does this look authentic to you?" — show a "maybe real, maybe fake" designer item. Expert debate in comments is the content.
15. "Depop girlies vs. eBay sellers — fight" — mock platform tribalism. Extremely shareable because people tag their reseller friends.
16. "This was in the donation bin behind the store — legal or not to take?" — genuine legal grey area. People will debate.
17. "Day [X] of asking followers to suggest a thrift category — today: [category]" — series that builds loyalty and commitment.
18. "I found your item" — tag a reseller in the video (ask permission first or use a hypothetical), then scan the item. "Is this the one you've been looking for?"
19. "One word that describes your resale niche — go" — simple comment prompt with insane participation because it's low effort.
20. "I'm scanning everything that costs under $5 in this Goodwill. Follow me." — ongoing thread engagement. Each follow-up references the original.
21. "The algorithm is suppressing thrift content — share this if you agree" — conspiracy/community energy drives shares even if debatable.

## 3. HOOK FORMULAS (15+ Templates)

Each hook below assumes text overlay format with no talking required. The hook appears in the first 1–2 seconds as a single bold text overlay on a visually interesting B-roll background (scanning hand, thrift rack, haul spread).

**Hook 1: "I paid $[low amount] for this."**
*Why it works:* It creates an open loop immediately. The brain asks: "For what? Is that a good deal or a bad one?" The dissonance between a low number and an implied value drives watch-through. The period (not a question mark) implies confidence, which is more intriguing than a question.

**Hook 2: "You're leaving money at the thrift store."**
*Why it works:* Direct accusation triggers defensiveness and then curiosity. "What am I missing?" is an irresistible internal question for anyone who thrifts. Loss aversion (leaving money) is psychologically stronger than gain framing ("make money").

**Hook 3: "Wait for the number."**
*Why it works:* Explicit instruction to wait creates anticipation. It also front-loads the payoff — the viewer now knows there's a specific "number" moment coming, so they stay to see it. Used with the scan reveal format, it makes the scan itself feel like a countdown.

**Hook 4: "The app said $[high amount]. I paid $[low amount]."**
*Why it works:* Leads with the punchline and the proof simultaneously. Nothing to interpret — the value is right there in the hook. The gap between the two numbers creates immediate "how" curiosity.

**Hook 5: "Nobody is talking about this brand."**
*Why it works:* Insider knowledge framing. "Nobody is talking about" implies the viewer is about to receive exclusive information. In the reseller community, information is competitive advantage, so this triggers both FOMO and the desire to save the video before anyone else does.

**Hook 6: "POV: You almost walked past $80."**
*Why it works:* POV format puts the viewer in the scenario. "Almost walked past" triggers near-miss psychology — the emotional weight of almost missing something is stronger than simply finding it. Highly relatable for any thrifter.

**Hook 7: "This [item] is worth more than you think."**
*Why it works:* Directly targets the viewer's assumptions about a specific item or category. "More than you think" implies the viewer is currently underestimating value, which is a mild but motivating form of intellectual challenge.

**Hook 8: "Scan it before you skip it."**
*Why it works:* Imperative verb ("scan it") creates urgency. "Before you skip it" implies past behavior the viewer has been doing wrong. It also directly introduces the app's core action without feeling like an ad because the framing is advice, not pitch.

**Hook 9: "3 items. $12 in. $[amount] out."**
*Why it works:* Pure math hook. Numbers are inherently specific and credible. The "in/out" structure implies a system, not luck, which is aspirational. Short enough to absorb in one glance.

**Hook 10: "I've been doing this wrong for [X] months."**
*Why it works:* Self-deprecating admission creates instant relatability and positions the viewer to feel smarter than the creator (briefly), which is a pleasant feeling that makes them stay for the twist. The reveal is always: "then I found [this method/this app]."

**Hook 11: "The thrift store doesn't want you to know this."**
*Why it works:* Conspiracy framing is psychologically irresistible. "Doesn't want you to know" implies institutional suppression of information, which triggers tribal "us vs. them" energy. In the reseller community, thrift stores raising prices is a genuine source of tension.

**Hook 12: "This is what $50 at Goodwill actually becomes."**
*Why it works:* "Actually becomes" implies the answer is different from what you'd expect. The word "actually" signals a correction to conventional wisdom. Starting with a specific number ($50) anchors the viewer to a relatable investment amount.

**Hook 13: "Red flag. Red flag. Red flag. ✓"**
*Why it works:* Pattern with a break. Three warnings followed by a resolution creates a micro-narrative in 1 second. The checkmark implies something passed — but what? The brain needs to know what survived the red flag test.

**Hook 14: "Found in the $1 bin."**
*Why it works:* Ultra-specific price point implies a specific discovery narrative. "$1 bin" triggers automatic context for anyone who thrifts — that's the bins, the highest-effort, highest-reward format. It signals this video will pay off.

**Hook 15: "This app just changed how I think about thrifting."**
*Why it works:* Cognitive shift language ("changed how I think") implies the viewer's thinking is about to be changed too. It's not a feature list — it's an experience claim. Much harder to ignore than "this app helps you scan items."

**Hook 16: "My profit for [month]: $[amount]."**
*Why it works:* Direct disclosure of real numbers in the first frame. Transparency is rare enough to be attention-grabbing. The specific month makes it feel like a report, not a pitch, which increases credibility.

**Hook 17: "I scanned [X] items at Goodwill. Here's what I kept."**
*Why it works:* Implies a curation process — the viewer is about to see what survived a quality filter. The word "kept" implies there were rejections, and viewers want to see what made the cut and why. Creates a game of "would I have kept these too?"

## 4. POSTING STRATEGY

### Optimal Posting Frequency

**For a new account in the first 30 days:** Post every single day, no exceptions. The algorithm prioritizes accounts that maintain consistent posting velocity during their growth phase. One video per day minimum; two per day is better if you can sustain quality. Do not bulk-post three videos in one day and then disappear for three days — the algorithm interprets that as inconsistency and reduces distribution.

**After 30 days:** Reduce to 5–6 videos per week if daily pace is unsustainable. Never drop below 4 per week in the first 90 days. Consistency of schedule matters more than total volume once you're past the initial growth window.

### Optimal Posting Times for Reseller Audience

Your audience is primarily Gen Z and millennial women, many of whom have day jobs or school. Their highest-activity windows are:

**TikTok:** 7–9am EST (morning commute/prep), 12–1pm EST (lunch break), 7–10pm EST (evening scroll wind-down). The 7–9pm slot consistently outperforms for reseller content because that's when people are listing items after work. Your content meets them exactly where they are.

**Instagram Reels:** 8–10am EST, 11am–1pm EST, and 7–9pm EST. Reels performs slightly better on weekends for lifestyle/hobby content, so schedule your best video of the week for Saturday 9–11am.

**YouTube Shorts:** Less time-sensitive than TikTok/Reels because Shorts are more likely to be surfaced by the recommendation engine hours or days after posting. Still default to 12pm or 7pm for consistency.

**Day-of-week guidance:** Tuesday through Friday are your strongest posting days. Sunday evenings are underrated for reseller content (people are prepping for the week and in a productive mindset). Avoid posting on Monday mornings — engagement is lowest and the video's first-hour performance window will be wasted.

### Cross-Posting Strategy

Cross-posting the exact same video to all three platforms is acceptable in the first 30 days when volume is your priority. After that, here's how to differentiate:

Remove TikTok watermarks before posting to Reels and Shorts — use SnapTik or a similar remover. Both Instagram and YouTube suppress watermarked content from other platforms.

For TikTok, you can add on-screen captions directly in the app or use a tool like CapCut. Keep them at the bottom 1/3 of the frame to avoid being covered by the UI.

For Reels, add more context in the caption since Instagram users read captions more than TikTok users. Write 3–5 lines in the Reels caption where you'd leave the TikTok caption as just hashtags.

For Shorts, the title field matters more than on the other platforms because YouTube has a search-discovery layer the others lack. Write titles like "AI scans thrift finds for resale value | ThriftVault" because someone might actually search that.

Film everything in 9:16 (vertical) with your main content in the center 80% of the frame so it displays correctly across all three platforms without cropping issues. Leave headroom at the top (45px) and foot room at the bottom (120px) to avoid UI overlap.

### Hashtag Strategy

On TikTok, use 5–8 hashtags total. More than that reads as spam to the algorithm. Mix:
- 2 ultra-niche hashtags with under 500K views: #thriftvault, #resellertok, #thriftflip2026
- 2 mid-tier hashtags with 1M–10M views: #thrifting, #depopseller, #resellercommunity
- 1–2 broad hashtags with 50M+ views: #thriftstore, #smallbusiness

On Reels, hashtags matter less than they did two years ago, but still use 5–8 in the caption. Prioritize relevance over volume — Instagram's algorithm now reads caption context, so adding #fyp #viral is largely wasted.

On Shorts, skip hashtags in the description almost entirely and focus your energy on the title and description text. YouTube's search and recommendation engine is keyword-driven, not hashtag-driven.

**Your owned hashtag:** Create #ThriftVaultApp from day one and use it in every single video. Encourage users to use it too. This becomes a searchable content hub as the account grows, and it's a signal to the algorithm that your content is part of a defined series.

## 5. VIRALITY TACTICS

### What Makes Reseller Content Go Viral vs. Flop

**Viral:** A specific, unbelievable number (paid $1, sold for $200). Real footage of a real scan with a real result. A brand or item the viewer has personal history with. Content that makes viewers feel like they discovered information before others. An emotional response that's shareable — shock, aspiration, pride, or the feeling of "I need to send this to my reseller friend."

**Flop:** Vague content without specific numbers. Motivational captions without proof. Staged or obviously scripted "authentic moments." Identical content to a thousand other accounts (generic thrift haul without a unique angle). A talking-head format without captions when your audience scrolls on mute. Content that only resellers can understand (too niche) or content too generic for resellers to care about (too broad).

The single fastest way to go viral in this niche is to show something that seems impossible and then prove it's real. The scan reveal format is purpose-built for this.

### Engineering Rewatchability in 10-Second Videos

**The loop trick:** End your video at a visual or text moment that flows back into the start. If your video begins with "I paid $2.99 for this" and ends on the scan result number, follow it with a one-frame cut back to the item. The viewer's brain re-enters the loop naturally and replays before consciously deciding to.

**Information density:** Pack more information per second than the viewer can fully absorb on one watch. If a single blink cut contains three pieces of text and two visual elements, the viewer will rewatch to catch what they missed. Each rewatch is a view count signal.

**Intentional ambiguity:** Leave one thing slightly unexplained. Not confusingly — just slightly open. "What brand was the jacket?" is enough. Viewers will rewatch looking for the answer, then comment when they can't find it.

**Sound design:** A sound that doesn't resolve until frame 8 of a 10-frame video will be replayed to hear the resolution again. Design your audio arc to peak at the very last second.

### Comment Section Strategies

Respond to every comment in the first hour after posting. The algorithm measures comment-response ratio as an engagement quality signal. A video with 20 comments and 20 replies outperforms a video with 40 comments and 0 replies.

**Pin a reply that asks a follow-up question.** After a profit reveal video, pin "Which item do you want me to rescan in a week to show the price shift?" This drives fresh comments days after the initial post.

**Create comment bait by leaving something out.** Don't tell viewers which platform you sold the item on. They'll ask. When they ask, reply with the platform name AND "Part 2 coming showing the listing." This creates a follow-up content thread.

**Seed the first comment yourself.** Post the video and immediately comment "The brand surprised me too" or "The number at the end gets me every time." Viewers see this comment before scrolling to the next video and it reframes their watch experience with heightened anticipation.

**Controversy lite:** Post a take that most people agree with but some will push back on ("Poshmark is slowing down for vintage" is debatable enough to generate both agreement and argument).

### Duet, Stitch, and Reply Strategies

**Stitch strategy:** Find reseller videos with high view counts that show an item being thrifted WITHOUT a clear understanding of its value. Stitch with "Let me scan this" energy — open the ThriftVault app and scan a similar item. You're riding their traffic and introducing your tool organically. Do this 3–4 times per week during your first 30 days.

**Duet strategy:** Duet other resellers' "would you buy this?" content specifically. Your frame shows you scanning a similar item in real time while their content plays. The contrast between "guessing" and "knowing" is the entire pitch.

**Reply video strategy:** When someone comments "how do you know how much something is worth?" — film a video reply where you open the app and demonstrate. Comment replies get priority distribution to the commenter's followers and create warm introduction to new audiences.

**Tag-and-challenge strategy:** Create a "What's it worth?" challenge format where you tag 2–3 reseller creators. They scan an item and tag back. This cross-pollination works especially well during launch because the challenge format feels fun rather than promotional.

### Profile Pins Strategy (3 Pinned Videos Maximum)

### Building from 0 to 1,000 Followers

The fastest path is combining consistent posting with active engagement in other people's comment sections. Before you have an audience, you need to borrow other people's.

Go into the comment sections of the top 20 reseller accounts and leave genuinely useful comments — not "nice video!" but "the brand on that jacket is [X], it resells for $60+ on Depop." Be the helpful expert in the room. When people click your profile after reading a good comment, your pinned videos convert them.

Follow and engage with 10–15 accounts per day in your niche during Week 1. Not spam-follows — real engagement with real comments. The algorithm surfaces your content to people who interact with similar accounts.

Collaborate early. DM 5 reseller creators with under 10K followers during Week 2 and offer to feature their finds in a scan video. Micro-creators are more likely to respond and share the collab than large accounts.

Use the "reply to comment with a video" feature aggressively. Every video reply you post gets distributed to the original commenter's audience, not just yours. This is the single highest-ROI growth tactic available on TikTok right now for small accounts.
